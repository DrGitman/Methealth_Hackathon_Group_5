HIV Essentials

What Is HIV?

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a virus that attacks the body’s immune system, making it harder to fight off infections and disease. If left untreated, HIV can lead to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome).

What Are the Symptoms?

There are several symptoms of HIV. Not everyone will have the same symptoms. It depends on the person and what stage of the disease they are in. Some symptoms include:

Fever

An elevated body temperature often with chills and night sweats.

Headache

Common during early stages alongside other flu‑like symptoms.

Muscle Aches & Joint Pain

Generalized body discomfort and stiffness in muscles or joints.

Rash

Flat or raised red spots, usually on the trunk or face.

Sore Throat & Mouth Sores

Pain or redness in the throat and painful ulcers in the mouth.

Swollen Lymph Glands

Enlarged nodes on the neck, armpits, or groin as your body fights infection.

Diarrhea

Persistent loose or watery stools lasting more than a few days.

Weight Loss

Unintended and significant loss of body weight over a short period.

Cough

A persistent, dry cough that doesn’t go away with standard remedies.

Night Sweats

Drenching sweats during sleep, often soaking clothing or sheets.

Who Can Get HIV?

Anyone can get HIV, no matter your age, your sex, gender or sexual orientation, or your race or ethnic origin.

How Does It Spread?

HIV can only be passed by these five body fluids: blood, semen (including pre-cum), rectal fluid, vaginal fluid, and breast milk. Transmission occurs when one of these fluids enters the bloodstream of an HIV-negative person through broken skin or mucous membranes.

HIV cannot be passed through healthy, unbroken skin.

Common transmission routes: Unprotected sex with an infected person, sharing needles or syringes, mother-to-child during pregnancy, birth, or breastfeeding, and blood transfusions (extremely rare with screening).

Non-transmission: HIV is not spread by shaking hands, working or eating with someone who has HIV, hugs or kisses, coughs or sneezes, swimming pools, toilet seats, water fountains, insects, or casual contact.

Rare cases: Oral sex in presence of sores or ejaculation, needlestick injuries in healthcare, pre-chewed food with infected blood, severe bite trauma, or unsafe cosmetic procedures with contaminated equipment.

How to Prevent HIV Infection

Use condoms correctly every time you have sex. Condoms are highly effective at preventing HIV and many STIs.

Abstain from sex for complete prevention of sexual transmission and other STIs.

PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis) is a daily medication for high-risk individuals to prevent HIV.

PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) taken within 72 hours of possible exposure can prevent infection.

Avoid sharing needles; use new, sterile equipment each time.

Know your partner’s HIV status and get tested regularly.

If you are pregnant, get tested early and take HIV medicine to prevent transmission to your baby.

Treatment

There is no cure for HIV, but treatment with antiretroviral therapy (ART) can reduce the virus in your body to undetectable levels, keeping you healthy and preventing transmission.

Start treatment as soon as possible after diagnosis. Pills are standard; long-acting shots are available for stable patients.

Maintain regular appointments and discuss side effects or interactions with your healthcare provider. ART is compatible with birth control and hormone therapy, but always consult your provider.

Common Myths

Myth: Mosquitoes can transmit HIV. Fact: Insects cannot carry the virus.

Myth: You can get HIV from hugging or sharing utensils. Fact: HIV is not spread through casual contact.

Myth: HIV has a cure. Fact: Current treatments control but do not eliminate the virus.

Stigma & Support

Stigma leads to isolation, depression, and barriers to treatment. Offer empathy, respect, and accurate information.

Join support groups and community programs to reduce stigma and find peer support.
